# WLRT: Scope, Capabilities, and Interpretation

Wave Liquidity Redistribution Theory (WLRT) is a theoretical and engineering framework for modeling financial markets as dynamic systems of liquidity redistribution in price space.

Within WLRT, liquidity is treated as the fundamental state variable, while price is considered a secondary observable emerging from the structure and evolution of the liquidity field.

The framework is asset-agnostic and applies across multiple market environments, including equity, derivatives, and cryptocurrency markets.

WLRT is not a trading strategy and does not prescribe specific actions such as entry, exit, or capital allocation. However, this does not imply lack of practical applicability.

Instead, WLRT operates as a structural diagnostic and constraint-based framework that:
— identifies admissible and inadmissible market states,
— detects fragility and instability,
— defines the boundaries of possible system evolution.

WLRT studies liquidity environments as structured dynamic systems, enabling the identification of phase boundaries in market behavior.

Through the analysis of liquidity redistribution and fragility dynamics, WLRT provides:
— early identification of increasing systemic fragility and conditions associated with potential crisis formation,
— structural detection of phase transition boundaries,
— early recognition of post-crisis stabilization and transition to a new development phase.

These capabilities allow the detection not only of instability formation, but also of the structural conditions under which a crisis phase has likely completed and a new development phase becomes admissible.

Importantly, these results do not rely on predictive models, but arise from the diagnostic interpretation of the system's structural state and its evolution within the liquidity field.

Based on WLRT, multiple applied systems, diagnostic tools, and execution frameworks have been developed and continue to evolve. These systems utilize WLRT as a structural layer for decision-making, risk control, and system design.

WLRT includes a formal mathematical layer consisting of:
— definitions of liquidity fields,
— structural invariants,
— diagnostic operators (including the Local Fragility Index, LFI),
— conditions for phase transitions and regime changes.

The mathematical structure of WLRT is actively developing and extends beyond purely descriptive models.

Thus, WLRT should not be interpreted as a non-applicable theoretical construct, but as a foundational framework defining the limits, structure, and admissible space of market behavior and execution.
