# What Type of Events Is Most Likely to Act as a “Boundary Crossing”

**From the perspective of Wave Liquidity Redistribution Theory (WLRT) and current conditions in Russia**  
**Date of the initial framework: December 27, 2025**

## First — the key WLRT principle

The most dangerous scenario is not the most “frightening” one, but the one for which the conditions are already almost prepared.

WLRT does not select:

- the loudest scenario;
- the most discussed scenario;
- the most political scenario.

It selects:

- the least compensable scenario.

## Brief answer

The most likely type of boundary-crossing event is one that acts through liquidity and expectations simultaneously, rather than through a single immediate “shock.”

In other words, not one large explosion, but a loss of the system’s ability to absorb ordinary actions.

## Why not “one major external shock”

From the WLRT perspective:

- major external shocks have already occurred;
- the system has learned to partially absorb them;
- such events already have protocols, responses, and experience around them.

This reduces their probability as an immediate boundary-crossing trigger.

They remain dangerous, but they are less likely to become the trigger by themselves unless accompanied by other factors.

## Why not an “instant internal collapse”

WLRT also does not consider the following to be the most likely scenario:

- a sudden one-time breakdown;
- an abrupt total shutdown.

Why:

- the system is still functioning;
- the market is still operating;
- exchange has not been destroyed.

The boundary has not yet been crossed.

## The most vulnerable scenario according to WLRT

**A combined scenario: liquidity compression + a shift in expectations.**

This is the key point.

## What this means in simple terms

Not:

> “something terrible happened”

But:

> “ordinary actions suddenly became dangerous”

## How this looks in reality: a typical mechanism

### 1. Liquidity gradually declines

- cautious lending;
- slower investment;
- growing precautionary savings;
- lower turnover.

Everything still looks “normal.”

### 2. A medium-scale event occurs

Not a catastrophe, but something like:

- a change in conditions;
- a local failure;
- a limited sectoral shock.

The event itself is bearable.

### 3. But it acts through expectations

- participants begin to expect deterioration;
- decisions are postponed;
- activity declines.

Exchange slows down even further.

### 4. The WLRT moment occurs

- a small action produces a strong reaction;
- the market stops absorbing steps;
- asymmetry rises sharply.

The boundary is crossed not because of the event itself, but because of the state of the system.

## Why this type is the most likely

### 1. The conditions are already partly present

- reduced liquidity;
- participant caution;
- dependence on expectations;
- limited alternatives.

### 2. It does not require extreme events

- no “black swan” is needed;
- an “ordinary” shock is sufficient.

### 3. It is difficult to observe in advance

- prices may remain stable;
- statistics may look “acceptable”;
- anxiety accumulates internally.

WLRT is designed precisely for such regimes.

## A very important difference from 1989–1991

Then:

- exchange physically disappeared;
- shortages were visible.

Now, potentially:

- exchange may suddenly stop working, although formally it still exists.

This is a quieter, but more insidious scenario.

## How WLRT formulates it

The most likely event is not a shock, but the loss of the system’s ability to absorb shocks.

## Conclusion

From the perspective of WLRT, under current conditions the most likely type of event capable of producing a boundary crossing is a combination of gradual liquidity compression and a sharp deterioration of expectations, triggered by a medium-scale event rather than by an extreme shock.

Such scenarios are dangerous precisely because they look “ordinary” until the moment when the market suddenly stops being a market.
