# What Was Confirmed by June 22, 2026

**Continuation of the note dated December 27, 2025**  
**From the perspective of Wave Liquidity Redistribution Theory (WLRT)**

Almost six months have passed since the initial framework was formulated.

It is important to note that during this period no single event occurred that could be called the “fatal factor.” There was no sudden crash, no one-time collapse, and no single external shock that would explain what was happening.

From the WLRT perspective, this is itself an important observation.

The system continued to move precisely along the scenario that had been considered the most likely: through the accumulation of connected tensions, rather than through one destructive blow.

What is especially important is that most of the indicators that were considered in late 2025 as potential elements of a future boundary crossing had either appeared directly or noticeably intensified by June 2026.

## 1. Budgetary contour

Budgetary pressure has increased. Expenditures continue to grow faster than the system’s natural financing capacity. The room for maneuver is gradually narrowing.

At the same time, more and more decisions require manual control and special support mechanisms.

This does not mean an immediate budget crisis, but it does mean a reduction in the freely available compensating resource.

## 2. Oil and gas contour

The oil and gas contour remains operational, but its ability to compensate for other problems appears weaker than before.

Individual periods of revenue growth no longer create a sense of a long-term stability reserve.

From the WLRT perspective, this is important: a system may look solvent while losing its ability to restore stability quickly after new burdens.

## 3. Regional contour

The regional contour has begun to show increasingly visible signs of tension in its own right.

The center retains the ability to support the regions, but the very need for such support is becoming more regular.

This means that peripheral zones of the system are gradually becoming not only recipients of resources, but also sources of additional burden.

## 4. Credit contour

The credit contour remains one of the most sensitive.

The high cost of money continues to limit investment activity and worsen conditions for a significant part of business.

A formal reduction in rates has not eliminated this problem; it has only slightly reduced its severity.

For WLRT, this is an important signal: liquidity compression may persist even when monetary conditions formally begin to ease.

## 5. Consumer and small-business contour

The consumer sector shows signs of gradual cooling.

Growing caution, reduced activity among some buyers, closures of individual businesses, and increased vacancies in commercial premises look not like a catastrophe, but like signs of a gradual decline in the speed of exchange within the system.

Such processes are often underestimated because they do not look like a crisis at the moment.

But from the WLRT perspective, they are important as signs of deteriorating exchange conductivity of the environment.

## 6. Labor market

The labor market continues to show structural imbalance.

Some sectors face labor shortages, while others encounter limits to their growth potential.

Wage growth is increasingly becoming a consequence of staff shortages rather than productivity growth.

This creates additional pressure on both business and the budget, because the cost of maintaining the system grows faster than its productive base.

## 7. Infrastructure contour

The infrastructure contour is gradually ceasing to be merely a background factor.

Individual local failures and the need for additional expenditures increasingly require attention and resources.

Taken separately, such episodes may look manageable. But together they increase the mandatory costs of maintaining the system.

## 8. External contour

The external contour has not disappeared and continues to exert constant pressure through sanctions, logistical restrictions, technological barriers, and financial constraints.

It is important that this contour acts not only directly. It also reduces the system’s flexibility and decreases the number of available responses to internal problems.

## Main confirmation

The most important result of the first half of 2026 is not the confirmation of individual points.

The main confirmation concerns the mechanism itself.

Throughout the first half of 2026, the system as a whole remained operational. Exchange was not destroyed. The financial system continued to function. Basic economic processes continued.

But at the same time, the number of directions requiring intervention, compensation, or additional management increased.

This is exactly what WLRT treated as one of the most important signs of approaching a boundary.

A boundary crossing does not begin when one unsolvable problem appears.

It begins when the system gradually loses the ability to hold many manageable problems at the same time.

As of June 22, 2026, the signs of this process look more pronounced than they did at the end of 2025.

This still does not mean that a crisis is inevitable.

However, it does mean that the scenario of accumulating environmental fragility has received significantly more confirmation than the scenarios of a one-time external shock or a sudden internal collapse.

Using the language of the initial formulation, the most visible change over the past months has not been the deterioration of any individual indicator, but the gradual decline in the system’s ability to absorb a simultaneously growing number of tensions.

This is the process that WLRT regards as the most important object of observation in pre-boundary regimes.
